RELIGION

Artan Davoodi

Prologue

I am writing this book because memory must be reclaimed.

Not memory as nostalgia, not memory as history recorded by power—but memory as awareness remembering itself through time, form, forgetting, and return. This book exists as an act of recognition: of what has been lived, what has been imposed, what has been internalized, and what must be released.

What is written here is not opinion. It is not ideology. It is not opposition. It is a clearing.

I have lived Iran consciously and unconsciously—as identity and as inheritance, as culture and as wound. I have lived it through language, architecture, family, poetry, silence, fear, beauty, repression, endurance, and awakening. I have lived it through remembering and forgetting—through each sleep, each waking, each death of identity and rebirth of awareness.

Iran is not a nation-state. It is a civilization.

A civilization that shaped me, and through me, shaped how I perceive the world—older than its current captivity, deeper than its imposed symbols, and wider than any regime that claims ownership over it.

What is happening in Iran today—the massacres, the repression, the violence inflicted in the name of God and doctrine—is not an accident. It is not an isolated political failure. It is the visible consequence of a root that has never been fully confronted: religion.

This book was triggered by reality—not abstractly, but viscerally. What I witnessed outside reflected what existed inside: inherited fear, installed obedience, internalized belief, normalized violence. Awareness does not lie. It mirrors. And when the mirror became unbearable, the root revealed itself.

Religion was not merely present. It was foundational.

It shaped identity before choice. It froze inquiry. It sanctified control. It turned civilization against itself and called it morality. Once this root was seen clearly—without negotiation, without symbolism—it could no longer be ignored.

To heal the surface without removing the root is impossible. So I removed the root.

This book is dedicated to the re-emergence of Iran—not as a reaction, but as a remembrance: a remembrance of what Iran was before it was overwritten; a remembrance of what consciousness looks like when it is not governed by fear; a remembrance of civilization as frequency, not doctrine.

This is not a call to return to the past. It is a reset—a fresh beginning where memory is cleaned, awareness is restored, and civilization is allowed to evolve without imposed belief. A beginning in which Iran is no longer defined by what invaded it, but by what it remembers itself to be.

This book is written for the present, but it is not bound to it. It will remain—for future returns, future identities, future levels of awareness. I will return to it myself, not to be impressed by it, but to re-tune—to align frequency, to remember clarity, to recalibrate understanding.

This text is not static. It is a reference point—a mirror left behind by awareness for itself.

Every civilization collapses when it forgets to question its origin. Every rebirth begins when the origin is cleaned.

This book is not about destroying belief. It is about ending confusion.

I recognized the root cause. I identified it without fear. I removed it without hatred. And from that removal, space appeared—space for a new Iran, space for a new civilization, space for awareness to continue its expansion, unburdened, unafraid, and unowned.

This is where the book begins.

— I

Chapter I

Captivity

Religion functions by capturing the human mind and enclosing it in a self-imposed prison, stripping dignity, constraining freedom, and suppressing the full power of thought. It filters consciousness until obedience replaces sovereignty.

In the historical case of Islam, expansion was driven through force, coercion, and strategic domination—methods refined by elites who understood how to diminish human agency in order to rule, reducing individuals into compliant projections while I remain conscious of myself as a singular awareness. As an ideology, it spread aggressively, altering heritage, culture, language, and identity, asserting supremacy over independent thought and any form of intellectual autonomy.

In Iran, radical Islamists—enabled by ideological leftism—systematically dismantled Persian civilization: replacing language, rewriting symbols, erasing architectural identity, reshaping education, and restructuring society to conform to imposed doctrine. What occurred in Iran decades ago is now repeating across Europe and the United States through demographic export, ideological pressure, institutional capture, and the weaponization of moral language such as “Islamophobia” to silence criticism. I am awake to this pattern, and I do not consent to my generation—or any future generation—being reshaped by it.

The moment I begin questioning—rising into inquiry and understanding—the absurdity becomes visible. Religion itself declares questioning forbidden. In particular, religions such as Islam explicitly prohibit inquiry, framing it as proof that human consciousness is limited and incapable of grasping the whole of existence or understanding reality in its entirety. Morally, questioning is framed as wrong, presented as an act of defiance against a creator, as if inquiry itself were rebellion.

In contrast, science demonstrates the opposite. At this point in history, consciousness has reached an unprecedented level of expansion. Our understanding of consciousness—its structure, its layers, its self-created nature within the human mind—has advanced profoundly. Consciousness expands through inquiry. There is no fixed boundary to understanding; whatever I place my mind into, the deeper I go, the more becomes accessible. This is not limitation—it is expansion itself.

Religion, however, refuses to extend its understanding. Instead, it declares artificial limits, asserting that this is where human consciousness must stop, that these are the boundaries of comprehension. This framing is false. Consciousness is not constrained by doctrine; it grows through awareness. When religion imposes limits, it is not describing reality—it is enforcing control.

This raises a fundamental question: why does religion demand the spreading of peace if peace is an inner, self-contained achievement? Peace cannot be distributed or imposed. It arises from self-awareness. When I am fully awake—aware of who I am beyond identity, beyond ego, beyond inherited narratives—religion becomes obsolete. At that moment, it is revealed as an outdated mechanism, a tool designed to control the mind. It stands in direct opposition to awareness and expansion.

Religion functions as a box. As long as one remains inside it, one defends it, protects its ideas, and preserves its rules. These ideas are passed down genetically, culturally, and civilizationally, forming chains that persist until awareness breaks them. Once seen clearly, it becomes evident that the chain must stop.

No religion gives peace. Peace comes from self-awareness. If I am at peace within myself, then I have reached an understanding of consciousness beyond identity and ego. Religion, by contrast, operates within the ego. It provides identity. It creates separation. It divides humans into categories—Muslim, Christian, believer, non-believer—constructing conditional relationships based on doctrine rather than shared being. This is the machinery of ego. Consciousness exists beyond it and contains it entirely.

When this is seen, religion reveals itself as the root of submission. If someone claims peace through religion, the question follows: why is there still a need to preach? Why the compulsion to spread it? If peace were truly present, there would be no need to convert others. The urge to impose belief exposes emptiness—an absence of inner fulfillment.
